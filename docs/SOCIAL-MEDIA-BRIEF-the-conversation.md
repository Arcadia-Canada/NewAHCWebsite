# SOCIAL-MEDIA-BRIEF — The Conversation You Keep Putting Off

Brief for the social media manager. Send once the article is live.

## What this article is

The second piece in Arcadia's Master Prompt series. The pillar (*When the Person You Love Needs More Help*) was about recognizing the situation has changed. This article is about the difficult conversations adult children avoid having with aging parents — about driving, money, declining ability, or accepting outside help.

It validates the avoidance, names why these conversations are so hard, and gives readers language and permission to start.

It is not a how-to listicle. It is not a script. It is a piece of writing that helps the reader feel less alone in their hesitation, then quietly opens a door.

## Three pull quotes for graphics

**Quote 1 — the category insight**
> The conversation you keep putting off is not a single conversation. It is a category. And almost every adult child in your situation is putting off some version of it.

**Quote 2 — the unspoken truth**
> Most adult children do not realize, until afterward, how much of their hesitation was their own grief.

**Quote 3 — the permission**
> Imperfectly, gently, on a Tuesday, when nothing in particular is happening — that is usually how the conversations that change things actually start.

Use these cyclically. Don't post all three in one week.

## Recommended post angles

### Lead with recognition
- *"You have been meaning to have this conversation for months. Maybe longer."* + URL
- *"You know exactly which conversation it is."* + URL
- *"The conversation about driving. About the unopened mail. About the offer of help your mother has refused three times."* + URL

### Lead with the hidden truth
- *"Your parent's anger is almost never about you. It is grief in the shape of refusal."* + URL
- *"You are not just having a hard conversation. You are also, quietly, grieving."* + URL
- *"The first try is rarely the conversation. It is the announcement that the conversation exists."* + URL

### Lead with practical reframing
- *"Most aging parents resist help because they hear it as 'you can't manage anymore.' What they need to hear is something different."* + URL
- *"Lead with what you have noticed, not what you have decided. Ask, do not announce."* + URL

## Audience notes

Same primary persona as the pillar:
- Adult daughter or son in their 40s, 50s, 60s
- Living in Toronto or the GTA
- Carrying more responsibility than they admit out loud
- Searching at odd hours

But this article catches them in a more specific moment: they have been *trying* to have a conversation and not quite getting there. They are stuck, not just overwhelmed. The voice should match that — gentler, more permission-giving, less urgent than the pillar.

Especially common readers for this piece:
- Someone who just hung up the phone after another conversation that did not happen
- Someone driving home from a visit thinking *I should have said something*
- Someone whose sibling has been pressuring them to "have the talk"

## Tone

Match the article. The voice is even softer than the pillar — this article is permission, not urgency.

**Avoid:**
- "Have the talk!" / "Don't wait!" / "Now is the time!"
- Listicle framing ("5 tips for talking to aging parents...")
- Anything that sounds like a coaching prompt
- "Pro tip:" or "Here's the thing:"

**Lean into:**
- Recognition language — "you've been meaning to..."
- Grief language — naming what's actually being grieved
- Permission language — "you don't have to do it perfectly"
- Specific tiny scenes — "the dent on the bumper they didn't mention"
