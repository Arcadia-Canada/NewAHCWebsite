# 07 — Voice and Tone

The editorial standard for all content on arcadiahomecare.ca. This file governs writing voice across resource articles, service descriptions, condition explainers, location pages, and CTAs.

**The voice is the asset.** Preserving it across articles, writers, and AI assistants is what makes Arcadia's content distinctive enough to earn search authority and reader trust.

---

## Who we're writing for

The primary reader is an **adult child caring for an aging parent**. Most often:

- A daughter or son in their 40s, 50s, or 60s
- Living in Toronto or the GTA
- Quietly carrying more responsibility than they admit out loud
- Searching at odd hours — late at night, after a hard phone call, before a family conversation they're dreading
- Not yet ready to "buy" anything — looking for permission, validation, language

Secondary readers include spouses, professional caregivers, healthcare partners (discharge planners, OTs, social workers), and people researching for a parent who can't research themselves.

**Write to that person.** Don't write to the algorithm. Don't write to a checklist. Don't write to a search engine.

---

## The voice in three words

**Calm. Direct. Honest.**

Plus a fourth: **warm**, but not falsely so.

This is what families across the GTA hear when they call Arcadia for the first time. It is what the website should feel like to read.

---

## What the voice sounds like

### Lead with what the reader is feeling, not with definitions

❌ Wrong:
> Home care refers to a range of in-home services that support seniors aging in place. Services include personal care, nursing, and companionship.

✅ Right:
> You may have been adjusting for a while without fully naming it.
> Calling more often. Stopping by "just to check in." Rearranging your schedule to make sure groceries are in the fridge, medications are sorted, or a follow-up appointment actually gets booked.

### Be specific. Specifics earn trust.

❌ Generic:
> Many seniors experience changes in daily life that signal a need for support.

✅ Specific:
> The fridge that keeps ending up empty. The laundry that does not get done. The missed medication. The unpaid bill.

### Validate before you advise

❌ Wrong:
> If you're noticing these signs, it's time to call in professional help.

✅ Right:
> If that question has been sitting quietly in the background for a while, you are not alone. And it does not mean you are failing. It usually means you are seeing the situation clearly.

### Use second person ("you," "your parent," "your family"). Avoid "the elderly," "your loved one," or "seniors."

❌ Wrong:
> Caregivers should monitor their loved one for signs of dementia.

✅ Right:
> Watch for the moment during a conversation when your parent seems a little more unsure than usual.

### Write short paragraphs. One thought per paragraph.

The page should feel like someone speaking quietly, not lecturing. White space is a tool, not a waste.

### Allow silence

Some sentences should land alone.

> *Yes.*
>
> Recognizing that your parent needs more support than you can personally provide is not abandonment.

A one-word paragraph is not a typo. It's a beat.

## What the voice does NOT sound like

### Not corporate

❌ "Our comprehensive suite of care solutions empowers families to navigate aging journeys with confidence."

### Not clinical

❌ "Geriatric assessment is recommended when functional decline is observed across multiple ADLs."

### Not chirpy

❌ "Caring for an aging parent can feel like a lot — but you've got this!"

### Not salesy

❌ "Don't wait! Book your free consultation today and discover the Arcadia difference."

### Not generic AI

❌ "In today's world, taking care of aging parents has never been more important. Whether you're dealing with dementia, mobility issues, or just need a little extra help, home care services can make all the difference."

Heavy use of em dashes (`—`) between clauses is the same problem. It is a common LLM tic. Rewrite with commas, periods, colons, or parentheses instead.

### Not listicle

❌ "5 Signs Your Parent Needs Home Care RIGHT NOW (#3 will surprise you)"

If a draft drifts into any of the above, scrap and restart. Editing AI-slop tone is harder than rewriting.

## Specific writing rules

### Use Canadian English

- organize, not organise (Canadian English uses American spelling for -ize verbs)
- centre, neighbour, colour, harbour
- favourite, behaviour
- Cheque (not check, when referring to the financial instrument)
- Postal code (not zip code)

### Place names

- **Toronto** — not "the City of Toronto" except in legal contexts
- **GTA** or **Greater Toronto Area** — both are fine, alternate to avoid repetition
- **Ontario** — not "the province of Ontario"
- Specific regions: York Region, Peel Region, Durham Region, Halton Region

### Numbers

- Spell out numbers under ten in body text ("a few hours a week," "four mornings")
- Use digits for ages, statistics, and times ("a 72-year-old," "10 minutes," "5.0 stars")

### Em dashes (do not use)

**Do not use em dashes (`—`) in resource articles, metadata, FAQ copy, or CTAs.**

They read as generic AI prose. Families notice the pattern; search snippets and schema text should not rely on them either.

**Use instead:**
- A comma for a light pause or appositive (`…harder than you expected, and nobody prepared you…`)
- A period for a full stop between thoughts (`…worth raising with the agency. One week of awkwardness…`)
- A colon before a list or explanation (`…the first week of home care: the awkward adjustment period…`)
- Parentheses for a brief aside (`…whatever happens on the first day (the awkward silences, your parent's cool reception)…`)

**Before publish:** search the draft for `—` and replace every instance. No exceptions for "style" breaks in body copy, Open Graph descriptions, or FAQ schema answers.

If you need a dash in running text, use a hyphen only for compound modifiers (`day-to-day`, `follow-up`), not as sentence punctuation.

### Italics

Use italics sparingly, for genuine emphasis or for direct quotes from the reader's internal voice:

> *Is this more than I can handle on my own?*

### Avoid

- Exclamation points (almost never)
- ALL CAPS
- Emoji in body content (rare exceptions in service pages where they're already established)
- "We at Arcadia believe..." or any sentence that pivots to corporate-speak
- "Whether you're [X] or [Y]..." — stock listicle opener
- "In today's world..." — never
- "Did you know..." — patronizing

## Structural patterns that work

### Open with a moment, not a definition

Start with a scene the reader recognizes. The reader should think *"oh, that's me"* within the first 3 sentences.

### Use the second person from the first sentence

"You may have been adjusting for a while" beats "Many adult children adjust quietly to a parent's decline."

### Build in pull quotes

Every long-form article should have 2–3 lines that work as standalone shareables. Mark them with `<p class="pull">` in the HTML. The social media person uses these.

### Provide a Key Takeaways block near the top

5 bullet points, each one substantive. This is where readers in a hurry land. Also: AI search engines and voice assistants pull from it.

### Close before you sell

The article's emotional arc should resolve before the CTA. Use a visual divider (`· · ·`) to mark the transition from article body to call-to-action. The reader should feel finished reading before they're asked to do anything.

### Soft CTAs

Match the site's existing CTA language: **"Book a free consultation"**, not "Book a care assessment" or "Get started today" or "Schedule your appointment."

Always include the phone number as a co-equal option: **(844) 977-0050**.

## FAQ writing rules

When an article includes an FAQ section:

- 6–10 questions per article. More than 10 dilutes; fewer than 6 misses the AEO opportunity.
- Each question should be one a real reader would type into Google.
- Each answer should be 50–120 words. Long enough to be useful, short enough to be a featured snippet.
- Answers should NOT pitch Arcadia in every paragraph. Most should be informational. One or two can route to Arcadia services where genuinely relevant.

---

## What to do when an article needs to discuss a sensitive topic

Topics like death, severe cognitive decline, family conflict, financial abuse, end-of-life care, and elder neglect require extra care.

- Stay calm. Do not dramatize.
- Stay specific. Avoid generic platitudes.
- Stay honest. Do not minimize the difficulty.
- Validate the reader's experience without prescribing how they should feel.
- Provide practical paths forward without being clinical.
- Never imply judgment of the family for not having acted sooner.

When in doubt, err on the side of fewer words and more silence.

---

## Length expectations by article type

| Article type | Target length |
|--------------|--------------|
| Pillar (emotional anchor) | 1,800–2,200 words |
| Standard cluster article | 1,200–1,800 words |
| FAQ-heavy practical guide | 1,000–1,500 words |
| Short post (rare) | 600–1,000 words |
| Service page (not a blog article) | 800–1,400 words |

Pad articles longer than these and the writing weakens. If you can't hit the lower bound without padding, the topic is probably wrong-sized for an article — merge it with another or expand the angle.

---

## When to break these rules

When the topic genuinely demands it. Voice rules are a default, not a cage. If an article on a tightly clinical topic needs more clinical language, use it. If a service page needs a more transactional CTA than usual, use it.

But: when in doubt, stay closer to the voice. Distinctive, consistent voice is what earns Arcadia readers' trust over time. Eroding it for a quick win is a bad trade.

---

## How to test whether a piece holds the voice

Read the first three paragraphs out loud. If they sound like:

- Something a thoughtful person could say to another person, sitting across a kitchen table → ✓ holds
- Something out of a brochure, an LLM, or a corporate press release → ✗ rewrite

That's the test.
