## What this article is

A room-by-room practical guide to making the home safer for a parent with dementia. This article catches the reader at the point where they have already started making small adjustments (hiding knives, checking the stove, locking doors) but have not yet systematized them. It validates what they are already doing and extends it into a framework.

It sits inside the Dementia & Memory Care cluster as the "environment" companion to the "behaviour" articles. Where the early-signs article validates what the reader is observing, and the driving article demands action on the road, this one meets the reader inside the home they already know is not quite safe enough.

The emotional entry point: You have probably already started doing safety checks without calling them that. Scanning the kitchen before you leave. Checking whether the front door is locked. Quietly moving the pills to a spot your parent will not accidentally double-dose from.

The key reframe: You are not overreacting. If something in the house worries you, it is probably worth changing. And these changes are not about stripping the home of normalcy; they are about making it possible for your parent to remain in that home longer.

## Tone

This is a "validation and structure" article. The reader is already acting; they need confirmation and organization, not alarm. The tone must be practical, grounded, and quietly reassuring without minimizing the real risks.

The biggest voice risk is drifting toward a clinical checklist or a product catalogue of grab bars and door sensors. The value is in the emotional framing: these changes protect your parent, but they also protect your peace of mind. And the article must name the harder truth: when the home cannot be made safe enough, that is useful information too.

**Avoid:**
- Listicle framing ("10 home safety must-haves for dementia patients")
- Product-catalogue tone (brand names, prices, shopping links)
- Clinical language ("environmental modifications for cognitive impairment")
- Alarmist urgency ("Your home is a danger zone")
- "You've got this!" motivational framing
- Exclamation points
- "We at Arcadia believe..." or any corporate pivot

**Lean into:**
- The specific small safety fixes the reader recognizes (hiding knives, checking locks, removing rugs)
- Validation: what you are already doing counts
- The practical room-by-room frame: kitchen, bathroom, exits, bedroom, general
- Wandering reframed as a brain searching, not a person being difficult
- The honest ending: when the adjustments stop being enough, that itself is the signal
