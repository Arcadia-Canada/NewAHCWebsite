## What this article is

Master Prompt #3 — fourth piece in the priority series. Catches the reader at the moment immediately after the decision: care has started, and the reality is harder, stranger, and more uncertain than they imagined. This is the gap between arranging care and living with it — the adjustment period nobody prepares families for.

The emotional entry point: The caregiver started Monday. Your parent is cooler than usual. The caregiver is still finding where things are kept. Nothing feels natural yet. You are second-guessing a decision you spent weeks making.

The key reframe: The first week of home care is not a verdict on whether you made the right decision. It is an adjustment — one that almost every family goes through, and one that resolves within two to four weeks when the fit is right. Awkwardness is not failure. It is how care starts.

## Tone

This article follows directly from the decision — the reader has already committed. The tone must validate the difficulty of the adjustment without inflaming doubt about the choice. Lead with recognition of the specific, textured strangeness of week one. Do not reassure generically; reassure specifically.

**Avoid:**
- "You've got this!" / motivational framing
- Implying the first week should be easy or smooth
- Framing that makes the reader feel they are failing if things are hard
- Listing tips without acknowledging the emotional weight first
- Exclamation points (default to none)
- "We at Arcadia believe..." or any pivot to corporate-speak

**Lean into:**
- The specific small details of week one (the stranger in the house, the parent who is too formal, the caregiver still figuring out the routine)
- The reframe: awkwardness is not failure, it is adjustment
- Permission to feel uncertain without taking action on that uncertainty yet
- The difference between adjustment and a real problem — specific, not vague
- Direct address — "you," "your parent," "your caregiver"
