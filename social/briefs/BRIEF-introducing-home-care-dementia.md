## What this article is

Practical strategy article for families who have decided care is needed but cannot get the parent to accept it. Different playbook than introducing care to a cognitively intact parent.

The emotional entry point: Her parent does not want help. Does not think they need it. May refuse to let a caregiver through the door. She has tried persuasion, logic, ultimatums. Nothing has worked. She is starting to wonder whether the problem is her parent, or her own approach. Both can be true at once.

The key reframe: A parent with dementia who refuses help is not being stubborn. The part of the brain that monitors function is itself affected. They genuinely do not believe anything is wrong — because the evaluator inside has gone offline. This changes everything about what to do next.

## Tone

Tone reminder: patient and concrete. The reader is exhausted, frustrated, possibly grieving. Avoid implying she has been doing it wrong. Lead with the science (anosognosia is a symptom, not stubbornness), then move to what works.

**Avoid:**
- "Don't miss this!" / "Must read!" / "Share if you agree!"
- "You've got this!" / motivational framing of any kind
- Listicle framing ("5 tips for...")
- Exclamation points (default to none)
- "We at Arcadia believe..." or any pivot to corporate-speak
- Stock platitudes the reader has heard a thousand times

**Lean into:**
- Recognition language — "You have been..."
- Specific tiny scenes — the small detail your reader will recognize
- Permission language — "You do not have to do it perfectly"
- Direct address — "you," "your parent," "your family"
- Honest specificity over generic encouragement
