## What this article is

Master Prompt #2 — third piece in the priority series. Catches the reader in the moment when family tension has surfaced around a parent's care: the uneven load, the sibling who thinks things are fine, the group chat that has gone quiet. Names the conflict clearly, validates that it is normal, and moves the reader toward a path forward.

The emotional entry point: One sibling is driving to appointments every week and fielding the 7am calls. Another visited last month and thinks their parent seems fine. The group chat has gone quiet because nobody knows how to say what they are all thinking. The nearby sibling is running out of patience — not with the parent, but with being the only one.

The key reframe: Sibling conflict over a parent's care looks like a values problem — someone doesn't care enough, someone is overreacting. It is almost never that. It is a perception problem: siblings are working from genuinely different pictures of the same person, formed in different conditions, updated at different rates. Once that is named, the argument shifts from accusation to information-sharing.

## Tone

This article sits closest to resentment of all the Master Prompt pieces so far — the specific resentment of someone carrying too much. The tone must validate that without inflaming it. Lead with recognition, not judgment. The article names conflict without taking sides.

**Avoid:**
- "Don't miss this!" / "Must read!" / "Share if you agree!"
- "You've got this!" / motivational framing
- Listicle framing ("5 tips for managing sibling conflict")
- Taking sides — the article is deliberately written for any sibling, not just the primary caregiver
- Exclamation points (default to none)
- "We at Arcadia believe..." or any pivot to corporate-speak
- Framing that implies one sibling is right and one is wrong

**Lean into:**
- Recognition language — "You are not imagining it"
- The specific texture of this kind of conflict (the quiet group chat, the uneven load)
- Permission to name what is happening without it becoming an accusation
- The reframe: this is a perception gap, not a values gap
- Direct address — "you," "your sibling," "your family"
